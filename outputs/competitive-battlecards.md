## 1. Bank Sweep Programs (JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Wells Fargo)

**What they are:** The default cash holding mechanism at custodian banks. Uninvested capital in brokerage and fund accounts is automatically swept into bank deposit accounts or affiliated money market funds. This is what happens to idle capital when nobody makes a decision.

**Pricing / Fee Structure:** No explicit fee to the sponsor — that's the point. The bank earns the spread. JPMorgan sweep accounts have paid as low as 0.01%–0.03% while the federal funds rate exceeded 5%. Morgan Stanley raised sweep rates to ~2% only after SEC scrutiny in 2024. Wells Fargo and Bank of America paid $60M combined to settle SEC charges over sweep practices in January 2025. The banks invest sweep deposits at institutional rates (4%+) and return a fraction to the depositor. The implicit cost to a $100M idle position: $3M–$4M/year in captured spread.

**Target Market:** Everyone with a brokerage or custody account. This is not a product sponsors choose — it's a product they default into.

**Where They Win:**

- Zero friction. Capital is already there.
- FDIC coverage up to standard limits ($250K per depositor per bank).
- No implementation timeline — it's already running.
- Brand trust. JPMorgan, Goldman, Morgan Stanley are names LPs recognize.

**Where HMG Wins:**

- Yield: HMG's blended ~3.93% gross (~3.43% net) vs. 0.01%–2.00% sweep. On $100M idle, that's $1.4M–$3.4M/year net advantage.
- FDIC optimization: IntraFi network provides multi-million dollar FDIC coverage across 3,000+ banks — not $250K at one bank.
- White-label branding: LPs see "[Sponsor Name] Capital Management," not a generic bank statement.
- LP portal: Real-time capital visibility through Schwab institutional infrastructure.
- Fiduciary standard: SEC-registered RIA partner managing under fiduciary duty — banks operate under suitability standard.
- The regulatory environment is turning against sweep programs. Lawsuits and SEC probes at JPMorgan, Morgan Stanley, Schwab, Wells Fargo, and Raymond James validate the thesis that sweep economics are broken for depositors.

**Killer Question:** "What is your sweep account yielding right now — and do you know what the bank is earning on that same capital?"

**Objection Handler — "We're fine with our bank sweep."**
"Most sponsors are — until they see the number. On your fund size, your sweep is generating roughly [X] per year. The same capital on our platform nets approximately [Y]. That's not a product pitch — it's $[Z] that belongs to your fund. Can I show you the math on your specific structure?"

## 2. Addepar

**What they are:** A portfolio management and reporting platform for RIAs, family offices, and wealth managers. $3.25B valuation (Series G, May 2025). Over $8 trillion in assets tracked on the platform across 1,300+ firms. They aggregate data, generate reports, and provide analytics across asset classes.

**Pricing / Fee Structure:** Custom enterprise pricing. Average revenue per customer ~$229K/year. Significantly more expensive than alternatives like Orion or Black Diamond. Add-ons (trading, Navigator projection tool) cost extra.

**Target Market:** Large RIAs, family offices, private banks, and wirehouses. Not PE/RE fund managers specifically — Addepar is built for wealth advisory, not fund cash management.

**Where They Win:**

- Best-in-class multi-asset reporting and analytics. Handles alternatives, private markets, and complex structures better than most platforms.
- Strong brand among institutional wealth managers.
- API integrations and data aggregation across custodians.
- Navigator tool for private market cash flow projections.

**Where HMG Wins:**

- Different problem entirely. Addepar reports on portfolios — HMG manages idle cash. Addepar tells you where your capital is; HMG makes it productive.
- Addepar has no treasury management function. No yield optimization, no FDIC sweep optimization, no capital call integration.
- No white-label branding for fund sponsors. Addepar is advisor-facing, not LP-facing.
- HMG is complementary, not competitive. A sponsor could use Addepar for reporting and HMG for treasury — they solve different problems.
- Cost comparison is misleading — Addepar is a cost center ($229K/year); HMG is a profit center (generates net yield exceeding its fees at any scale above $25M).

**Killer Question:** "Addepar shows you where your cash is sitting. Who's managing what it earns while it sits there?"

**Objection Handler — "We already use Addepar."**
"Good — Addepar is excellent at what it does. But it's a reporting platform, not a treasury platform. It tracks your idle capital; it doesn't optimize it. We're not replacing Addepar — we're filling the gap between 'capital accounted for' and 'capital working.' What does your Addepar dashboard show for yield on uninvested cash right now?"

## 3. IntraFi Network (CDARS / ICS)

**What they are:** The largest FDIC deposit optimization network in the U.S. $525M revenue, $415M profit, ~80% margins. Owned by Warburg Pincus and Blackstone. Spreads deposits across 3,000+ FDIC-member banks to maximize insurance coverage. ICS handles demand deposits and money market accounts; CDARS handles CDs with maturities from 4 weeks to 3 years.

**Pricing / Fee Structure:** IntraFi earns a spread on placed deposits. No direct fee to the end depositor — the economics are embedded in the rate. Rates vary by participating bank and deposit size. CD rates published on their hub. The depositor works through a single bank relationship to access the network.

**Target Market:** Banks and financial institutions. IntraFi is a B2B infrastructure network — not directly accessible to PE sponsors or fund managers. A sponsor cannot open an IntraFi account; they access it through a bank or platform that's already integrated.

**Where They Win (Going Direct Through a Bank):**

- Maximum FDIC coverage — millions in aggregate insurance.
- Simple, single-bank relationship for the depositor.
- Trusted infrastructure — been operating since 2003 (as Promontory Interfinancial Network).
- CD options provide term yield premium over demand deposits.

**Where HMG Wins (vs. Going Direct):**

- Access barrier: Sponsors can't access IntraFi directly. They need a bank relationship and an intermediary. HMG has already built the integration.
- IntraFi is one component — HMG is the system. HMG uses IntraFi for the FDIC sweep tier (~35% of portfolio) but also deploys capital across T-bill ladders, agency notes, and ultrashort bond ETFs for a higher blended yield.
- No investment management: IntraFi places deposits. It doesn't manage a portfolio, optimize duration, or provide RIA oversight under fiduciary standard.
- No LP-facing infrastructure: No white-label portal, no branded reporting, no capital call integration.
- No reinvestment pathway: IntraFi doesn't connect Fund III distributions to Fund IV commitments.

**Killer Question:** "IntraFi handles FDIC optimization. Who handles the other 65% of your idle capital — the T-bill ladder, the duration management, the LP reporting?"

**Objection Handler — "Why wouldn't we just go to IntraFi directly?"**
"IntraFi is a bank-to-bank network — you can't access it directly as a fund manager. You'd need a bank intermediary. We've already integrated IntraFi into our platform for the FDIC tier, but IntraFi only solves one piece: deposit insurance. It doesn't manage a portfolio, optimize yield across duration, or give your LPs a branded portal. We use IntraFi — it's one of our infrastructure layers. The question is who manages the full picture."

## 4. Voya Financial

**What they are:** A financial services company focused on retirement solutions, investment management, and employee benefits. $7B+ market cap. Primary business is 401(k) administration, annuities, and institutional investment management. They offer government money market funds and institutional liquidity products.

**Pricing / Fee Structure:** Institutional money market funds with standard expense ratios (typically 15–30bps for institutional share classes). 401(k) and retirement platform fees are separate. Custom pricing for institutional cash management mandates — not publicly disclosed.

**Target Market:** Corporations, retirement plan sponsors, and institutional investors. Voya's institutional business is retirement-centric, not PE/RE fund-centric. They manage workplace savings, not fund idle capital.

**Where They Win:**

- Deep institutional presence in retirement and employee benefits.
- Government money market funds with competitive yields.
- Scale — $800B+ in assets under management and administration.
- Established relationships with large corporate treasurers.

**Where HMG Wins:**

- Wrong problem, wrong client. Voya manages retirement assets and corporate treasury. PE/RE fund idle capital has fundamentally different dynamics — capital calls, deployment cycles, inter-fund flows, LP reporting.
- No fund-specific infrastructure: No capital call integration, no LPA-aware cash management, no GP operational authority framework.
- No white-label: Voya is its own brand. No sponsor-branded LP experience.
- No FDIC optimization network: Voya's money market products are not FDIC-insured.
- Fiduciary gap: Voya's products are investment products, not a managed fiduciary platform under RIA oversight tailored to fund structures.

**Killer Question:** "Does your cash management solution understand the difference between committed-but-uncalled capital and post-exit pre-distribution cash? Because they have very different liquidity profiles."

**Objection Handler — "We use Voya for our institutional cash."**
"Voya is strong in retirement and corporate treasury. But fund idle capital is a different animal — your cash has deployment timelines, capital call schedules, and LP reporting requirements that a generic institutional money market doesn't address. We built specifically for the PE/RE fund lifecycle. Can I show you how the platform handles capital call liquidity versus longer-duration positioning?"

## 5. BlackRock Liquid Funds / Money Market Alternatives

**What they are:** The world's largest asset manager ($11.5T+ AUM) offers a suite of institutional liquidity funds, money market funds, and ultrashort bond strategies. BlackRock Liquidity Funds include government, treasury, and prime money market options. Their institutional shares carry contractual expense caps of ~0.17% through June 2026.

**Pricing / Fee Structure:** Institutional share class expense ratios: ~0.17% (liquidity funds) to ~0.50% (government money market portfolio). No entry/exit fees on most share classes. Yields track federal funds rate minus expenses — currently ~4.0%+ gross on institutional government money market.

**Target Market:** Institutional investors, corporate treasurers, RIAs, and anyone with $1M+ seeking cash-equivalent exposure. BlackRock's cash management division serves the broadest possible institutional market.

**Where They Win:**

- Yield: Institutional money market funds yield ~4%+ gross. Competitive with or slightly above HMG's blended gross on the liquid tier.
- Brand and scale: BlackRock is the default institutional name. LPs recognize it instantly.
- Liquidity: Same-day or T+1 redemption on most products.
- Low explicit cost: 17bps expense ratio is below HMG's 50bps AUM fee.
- No implementation friction: Buy the fund, done.

**Where HMG Wins:**

- Single instrument vs. system: A BlackRock money market fund is one product. HMG is a three-tier portfolio (liquid, intermediate, term) with a blended yield ~3.93% gross that outperforms a single money market in most rate environments.
- No FDIC coverage: Money market funds carry floating NAV risk. Not FDIC-insured. HMG's platform includes FDIC-optimized deposits via IntraFi.
- No LP infrastructure: No branded portal, no real-time capital visibility, no LP reporting integration. LPs see a generic fund statement.
- No capital call integration: No mechanism to coordinate liquidity with deployment schedules.
- No reinvestment pathway: No connection between Fund III distributions and Fund IV commitments.
- No fiduciary management: Buying a fund is a product decision. HMG provides RIA-managed portfolio oversight under fiduciary standard.
- The infrastructure signal: Using the same money market as a retail investor tells LPs nothing about operational sophistication. The platform tells a story.

**Killer Question:** "Your LPs have exposure to Blackstone's treasury operation on one line of their statement and a retail money market fund on yours. What story does that tell in your next fundraise?"

**Objection Handler — "We just use BlackRock money market funds."**
"BlackRock makes excellent money market products — we actually use similar instruments in our liquid tier. But a money market fund is a jar. It holds cash. It doesn't manage duration, optimize FDIC coverage, provide LP visibility, or brand the experience under your name. The yield difference between BlackRock and our liquid tier is marginal. The infrastructure difference is not. Let me show you what the full platform looks like compared to a single fund position."

## 6. Flourish Cash (MassMutual)

**What they are:** A cash management platform for RIAs and wealth managers. Acquired by MassMutual in 2021. Over $8B in assets under custody across 1,100+ advisory firms representing $2.6T in AUM. Offers high-yield savings through FDIC-insured program banks, plus annuities and lending products.

**Pricing / Fee Structure:** No explicit fee to the advisor or client. Flourish earns revenue through the interest rate spread between what program banks pay and what depositors receive. Current rate: ~3.25% APY (as of late 2025). This is below HMG's net yield of ~3.43%.

**Target Market:** RIAs and wealth advisors managing individual and HNW client cash. Retail and high-net-worth wealth management — not institutional PE/RE fund sponsors.

**Where They Win:**

- Simple, no-fee model for advisors. Easy to implement.
- FDIC coverage through multiple program banks.
- Growing platform with MassMutual backing.
- Expanding into lending and annuities — becoming a multi-product platform for RIAs.
- Strong advisor adoption (1,100+ firms).

**Where HMG Wins:**

- Wrong client, wrong problem. Flourish is built for individual wealth management cash. PE/RE fund idle capital has different dynamics: capital calls, deployment timelines, inter-fund flows, LP reporting requirements.
- Yield: HMG's blended ~3.43% net exceeds Flourish's ~3.25% APY, and HMG's portfolio includes duration and credit optimization that Flourish doesn't offer.
- No white-label branding for fund sponsors. LPs see Flourish, not the sponsor's brand.
- No capital call integration or LP reinvestment pathway.
- No T-bill ladder, no ultrashort bond allocation, no duration management — Flourish is deposits only.
- No institutional portal. Flourish's interface is built for individual account holders, not LP reporting.

**Killer Question:** "Flourish works well for your personal wealth clients. But does it handle capital calls, deployment schedules, and LP-level reporting for your fund?"

**Objection Handler — "We already use Flourish."**
"Flourish is solid for RIA client cash management — it's a good product for that use case. But your fund's idle capital is a different problem. Flourish doesn't do capital call integration, doesn't offer a sponsor-branded LP portal, and doesn't manage across duration tiers. It's a savings account platform; we're a treasury management platform. They solve different problems for different pools of capital."

## 7. Treasure Financial

**What they are:** A business treasury platform for operating companies and startups. SEC-registered RIA. Offers money market fund selection, T-bill and TIPS investing, and a managed income product. API-first approach targeting fintech-forward businesses.

**Pricing / Fee Structure:** Minimum $2M deposit to access promotional rates. Fee structure not publicly disclosed but likely a management fee on AUM plus potential performance component on managed income product. SOC-2 certified, GDPR compliant.

**Target Market:** Operating companies and startups with surplus business cash. Corporate treasurers looking for yield on operating reserves. Not PE/RE fund sponsors.

**Where They Win:**

- Clean, modern platform. API-first approach appeals to tech-forward companies.
- SEC-registered RIA with fiduciary duty — similar positioning to HMG's RIA partner.
- Diversified product set: money markets, T-bills, TIPS, managed income.
- Low minimum ($2M) makes it accessible to smaller businesses.

**Where HMG Wins:**

- Structural mismatch: Treasure manages operating company cash. Fund idle capital has fundamentally different cash flow dynamics — committed capital that hasn't been called, deployment timing, inter-fund flows, LP distributions.
- No LP-facing infrastructure: No white-label portal, no LP reporting, no branded experience.
- No capital call integration or fund lifecycle awareness.
- No FDIC optimization network (IntraFi integration).
- No Schwab custody infrastructure or institutional portal.
- No reinvestment pathway connecting fund distributions to next vehicle commitments.

**Killer Question:** "Treasure is built for a company's operating cash. Your fund has committed-but-uncalled capital, deployment cycles, and LP distributions. Who manages that?"

**Objection Handler — "We use Treasure for our business cash."**
"Treasure is a good product for operating company cash — and it might be the right tool for your management company's operating account. But fund idle capital is a different asset pool with different rules. Capital calls, LP reporting, deployment schedules, and the branded institutional experience your LPs expect — Treasure wasn't built for that. We were. You could use both: Treasure for the management company, HMG for the fund."

## 8. DIY / Internal Treasury Hire

**What they are:** Building an in-house treasury function by hiring a dedicated professional or assigning the CFO/COO to manage idle capital allocations. Full control, full customization, full overhead.

**Pricing / Fee Structure:** Qualified treasury hire: $150K–$300K fully loaded annually. Infrastructure (Bloomberg terminal, FDIC tools, money market access, reporting): $50K–$150K/year. Total in-house cost: $200K–$450K/year before a single dollar is optimized. HMG equivalent for $500M fund: ~$550K/year but generates $3.4M+ in net yield.

**Target Market:** Funds with $1B+ in idle capital where the fixed overhead is justified by scale. Not economical below that threshold.

**Where They Win:**

- Full control over investment decisions and timing.
- No third-party dependency or platform risk.
- Custom risk parameters and investment policy.
- Dedicated attention — not sharing a platform with other sponsors.

**Where HMG Wins:**

- Economics: In-house requires $1B+ idle capital to justify. HMG is net positive at $25M.
- Time to market: In-house build is 6–12 months (hire, infrastructure, compliance). HMG implementation: 3–8 weeks.
- Compliance burden: In-house means managing your own RIA registration or investment adviser exemption. HMG provides SEC-registered RIA oversight.
- Technology: Building an LP portal, FDIC optimization integration, and reporting infrastructure from scratch is a multi-year, multi-hundred-thousand-dollar project. HMG delivers it on Schwab infrastructure.
- Platform economics improve with scale across HMG's entire client base — costs that would be fixed for an in-house build are amortized.

**Killer Question:** "What would it cost you to build what Blackstone has for treasury — the team, the technology, the compliance infrastructure? And at your fund size, does that math work?"

**Objection Handler — "We're considering hiring a treasury person."**
"That can work at scale — Blackstone, KKR, and Apollo all have dedicated treasury teams. The question is whether your fund size justifies $200K–$450K in annual overhead for something that takes 6–12 months to build. We deliver the same outcome in 4–8 weeks at a fraction of the cost. And if you do eventually hire a treasury professional, the platform gives them a running start — they're managing a system, not building one."

## The Universal Opener

Regardless of what a prospect currently uses, the conversation starts the same way:

*"You've built a sophisticated operation for deploying capital. What happens to the capital that isn't deployed yet?"*

If they have an answer, drill into the economics. If they don't, you've already won the frame.
